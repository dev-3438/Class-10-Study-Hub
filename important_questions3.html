Question 1

3 Marks

What is heredity? Explain with an example.

Answer:

Heredity is the process by which characteristics (traits) are transferred from parents to their offspring through genes. It occurs because genetic information is passed through DNA present in gametes. Traits such as eye colour, hair type, height, and blood group are inherited from one generation to the next. Example: If both parents have curly hair, their child may also inherit curly hair because the gene for curliness is passed on.

Question 2

3 Marks

Differentiate between genotype and phenotype.

Genotype:
– Refers to the genetic constitution or the set of genes an organism carries.
– Represented using symbols like TT, Tt, tt.
– Inherited from parents and not directly observable.

Phenotype:
– Refers to the observable physical traits such as height or colour.
– Influenced by genotype + environment.
– Example: A plant appearing tall or short.

Question 3

3 Marks

State Mendel’s Law of Dominance.

Answer:

Mendel’s Law of Dominance states that when two contrasting alleles are present, only the dominant allele expresses itself, while the recessive allele remains hidden.
The dominant allele masks the recessive allele in the F₁ generation.
Example: In pea plants, tallness (T) is dominant over dwarfness (t).
So plants with TT or Tt genotype will appear tall.

Answer:

It helps survival, adaptability, and evolution.

Question 6

3 Marks

What are homologous and analogous organs? Give examples.

Answer:

Homologous: Same structure, different function. (Human arm & bat wing)
Analogous: Different structure, same function. (Bird wing & insect wing)

Question 7

4 Marks

What is natural selection? Explain with an example.

Answer:

Natural selection is the survival of individuals with advantageous traits.
Example: Dark peppered moths survived better in polluted areas.

Question 9

3 Marks

What is a Punnett square? How does it help in genetics?

Answer:

A Punnett square is a diagram used to predict possible allele combinations.
It helps determine genotype ratio, phenotype ratio, and probabilities.

Question 10

3 Marks

State Mendel’s Law of Segregation.

Answer:

During gamete formation, allele pairs separate so each gamete receives only one allele. Alleles remain pure and do not mix.
